# Critical assessment of the three theme pages

Reference points: the June 2026 outline and the revised version (Claude.pdf). The revised version reframes the project around a single conceptual move — **probing and stewardship as a co-constitutive practice of designing *with* institutions** — and identifies the **relational quality of public sector work** as the connecting concern across all four cases. The three theme pages do not yet fully carry that reframing.

---

## Cross-cutting issues

1. **The co-constitutive relation between probing and stewarding is not visible.** Stewarding change reads as a stand-alone interpersonal practice (navigating resistance, building trust). The revised framing makes the dependency explicit: probing without stewardship loses what it uncovered; stewardship without probing surfaces structure only for the surfacing to dissipate. This pairing is the central conceptual move and ought to appear on at least two of the three theme pages.
2. **The "designing with institutions" frame is referenced as a project name, but the underlying conceptual move is not surfaced** — that institutions are constituted through everyday practices, so design intervenes by reconfiguring those practices.
3. **The "relational quality of public sector work" is the connecting tissue** introduced in the revised text. It belongs on the caring-organisations page as the explicit umbrella, replacing the narrower "care ethics" framing the original page leans on.
4. **The neoliberalism / institutional-logics question** is hinted at in the public-design-practices page but stays under-developed. The revised version treats it as a politically fraught condition for the whole project, not a side-note: structures being probed are logics that have proven adept at incorporating critique into their own legitimation. A single sentence acknowledging this on the relevant pages would carry it.
5. **Minor language hygiene.** A few constructions to revise across the pages: the hyphen "-" in the stewarding-change description should be an em-dash; "carrying its own logics" (public design practices) is a number-agreement slip; "do good work" (caring organisations) is colloquial. UK spelling and the avoidance of "is not X, it is Y" constructions is generally already observed; the revisions below keep that intact.

## Public design practices

### What works

The pairing of design and public administration as practices with distinct vocabularies sets up the question well. "Promising and generative but also unstable" is the right tonal register. The four-competency framing and the "transversal and complementary" characterisation align with the Ch.1 conceptualisation in the revised version. The closing question ("does design's claimed transformative potential hold, or does it tend to reproduce the institutional logics it sets out to challenge?") is one of the strongest sentences across the three pages and lands the reader well into Stewarding change.

### What misaligns

The middle paragraph is dense: a six-item list of public sector practices inside an em-dash, followed by "carrying its own logics" (the singular "field" makes that read as a number-agreement slip). The repertoire's "may need to adapt to a context" softens what the revised framing sharpens: the repertoire **acquires new meaning** as it moves into the political-administrative domain. The revised version's two structural relationships (co-evolutionary search for perspective on action; navigation of conflict and tension) and three contextual movements (idealising/realising, complexifying/concretising, contesting/legitimising) are not present, even at a gesture; one or two could be introduced without inflating the page.

### Suggested revision

```markdown
---
title: "Public design practices"
description: "Examining what design practices bring when collaborating on complex societal challenges, and how that repertoire acquires new meaning in political-administrative contexts."
gradientFrom: "#FF4500"
gradientTo: "#FF9E00"
weight: 10
---

Design and public administration have developed distinct practices and vocabularies when working towards change. When they meet around complex societal challenges — climate change, housing shortages, social welfare, organised crime, and many more — the encounter is at once promising and unstable. Promising, because design's experimental and relational repertoire opens space for working with situations that resist analytical resolution. Unstable, because that repertoire was developed elsewhere and must acquire new meaning as it travels into a domain organised around bureaucratic, regulatory, and political logics.

In the project {{< project-link href="/projects/defining-design-practices-for-societal-challenges" value="Defining design practices for societal challenges" >}} we mapped an initial set of competencies, further detailed in {{< publication-link href="/publications/what-do-designers-bring-to-the-table" value="What do designers bring to the table?" >}}. Four competencies emerged — integrating, reframing, formgiving, and orchestrating — framed at a level of abstraction that can span varied applications while remaining grounded in expertise. Two structural relationships between them — the co-evolutionary search for a perspective on action, and the navigation of conflict and tension — describe what makes design practice distinctive when working in ill-defined, contested situations.

The public sector is a field of interconnected practices: politics, policymaking, administration, service delivery, enforcement, and communication, each carrying its own logics. Design enters this field as a transversal practice, cutting across these practices and connecting them rather than standing in service to any single one. A central question is what happens to design's repertoire as it moves into this domain: does its claimed transformative potential hold, or does it tend to reproduce the institutional logics it sets out to challenge? How that repertoire is carried through the conditions of organisational life is the work of {{< theme-link href="/themes/stewarding-change" value="Stewarding change" >}}.
```

## Stewarding change

### What works

The framing of the gap between prototype and embedded change is well posed. The cross-link to "Designing with institutions" (the project) and "From ideas to change" (the publication) maps the empirical and conceptual ground neatly. The closing pointer to caring-organisations gestures at what is at stake.

### What misaligns (most important of the three pages)

The page treats stewardship as a stand-alone, interpersonal practice — "navigating resistance, building trust, translating intentions" — which softens what the revised version makes specific. Two consequences follow.

First, **probing is missing entirely.** The revised version makes probing and stewardship co-constitutive aspects of the same practice. Stewarding change without probing is not what the dissertation is now arguing for. The chapter's organising concept needs to appear on the page that bears its name.

Second, the **definition of stewarding change is too generic.** The revised version's formulation — "the deliberate, attentive work of moving a design initiative through organisational structures and contingent events while remaining alert to what is being reproduced and what is being transformed" — does more conceptual work than "navigating resistance, building trust." The reproduction-versus-transformation alertness is what makes stewardship politically meaningful in a neoliberalised public sector; without it the concept collapses into project-management vocabulary.

Minor: the hyphen in the description should be an em-dash. "From ideas to change" is positioned as having "laid groundwork" for the Ch.3 work, which is accurate but reads as slightly self-effacing about the new longitudinal study; a tighter formulation makes the continuity sharper.

### Suggested revision

```markdown
---
title: "Stewarding change"
description: "Following design interventions through organisational life — examining the co-constitutive work of probing entrenched structures and carrying forward what they open up."
gradientFrom: "#F3E84F"
gradientTo: "#7EC86A"
weight: 20
---

Collaborations between designers and public sector organisations frequently produce outcomes that are meaningful as prototypes but never become embedded in practice. The gap between concept and lasting change reflects the entrenched character of the structures design seeks to engage — webs of regulations, IT systems, professional dispositions, relational patterns, and worldviews that remain largely illegible to those working within them. The research question follows: under what conditions can design practices disclose these structures and carry forward what those encounters open up?

Two moves describe this work. Probing is the designerly capacity to render structure perceivable by producing concrete encounters with it — a prototype placed into an organisational setting, a workshop that asks professionals to work differently for an afternoon, a service mock-up that maps existing systems by how they respond, resist, absorb, or deflect. Stewarding change is the deliberate, attentive carrying forward of a design initiative through organisational structures and contingent events, involving ongoing decisions about how to reframe, redirect, sustain, or let go of intentions as obstacles and opportunities emerge — while remaining alert to what is being reproduced and what is being transformed.

These moves are co-constitutive. Probing without stewardship loses sight of what it uncovers; stewardship without probing surfaces structures only for the surfacing to dissipate. The project {{< project-link href="/projects/designing-with-institutions" value="Designing with institutions" >}} follows this work in real time across three public design initiatives, examining how trajectories are jointly shaped by deliberate action and organisational response. In {{< publication-link href="/publications/from-ideas-to-change" value="From ideas to change" >}} we identified the recurring implementation challenges and practices designers develop to navigate them — an initial mapping that the longitudinal work extends and complicates. What is ultimately at stake in those efforts is the relational quality of public sector work, taken up in {{< theme-link href="/themes/caring-organisations-and-services" value="Caring organisations & services" >}}.
```

## Caring organisations & services

### What works

The NPM critique is well-pitched: control, audit, contractual accountability, narrowed professional judgement, eroded relational conditions for care. The closing move — design interventions working at the level of structures, not to optimise but to open space — aligns with Ch.4-5 of the revised version (probing governance structures; negotiating transformative change).

### What misaligns

The biggest tension is conceptual: the description and opening paragraph anchor the theme in a **"care ethics perspective,"** while the revised version has shifted the umbrella concern to **the relational quality of public sector work** — "the conditions under which professionals and the people they work with can meet each other as more than role-bearers within a procedure." This is a meaningfully broader framing that, in the revised version, ties together all four cases (Kopkrakers in the social domain, Fair Practice Lab in the cultural sector, Rijksmakerstaat in infrastructure, UWV in occupational disability). The current page reads as if the theme is the occupational disability case plus an NPM critique. The relational-quality framing keeps the disability case as the empirical anchor but allows the theme to do the umbrella work the revised version asks of it.

A second, smaller point: "what care requires of organisations" and "people to do good work" are slightly soft. "What the relational quality of public sector work requires" and "do work of quality" (or similar) are closer to the register the revised version sets.

A note on the title and slug: if the umbrella shifts to the relational quality of public sector work, the title "Caring organisations & services" may eventually feel narrow. Worth considering, though changing the slug breaks URLs.

### Suggested revision

```markdown
---
title: "Caring organisations & services"
description: "Engaging the relational quality of public sector work — the conditions under which professionals and the people they work with can meet each other as more than role-bearers within a procedure."
gradientFrom: "#FF9EC4"
gradientTo: "#A855F7"
weight: 30
---

A common texture appears in how pressure on Dutch public sector organisations is experienced. Across very different domains — social services, the cultural sector, infrastructure, occupational disability — professionals find their craft, agency, and capacity for care steadily constrained by regulatory arrangements whose origins and logics are difficult to trace. Citizens navigating benefits, makers seeking fair conditions, and colleagues attempting to do work that matters encounter the same arrangements from the other side. What gets eroded is the relational quality of these encounters: the conditions under which a professional and the person in front of them can meet each other as more than role-bearers in a procedure.

These conditions emerged from successive waves of structural reform sedimented across decades, rooted in worldviews informed by neoliberalism and their practical embedding in governance arrangements such as New Public Management — market mechanisms, principal-agent relationships, contractual arrangements with financial incentives and sanctions, and regimes of audit and accountability. The question this theme pursues is how design interventions can work at the level of these structures: rendering them perceivable, understanding how they shape agency, and opening space — within and against existing arrangements — for more attentive ways of working.

The work draws on a specific case — citizens navigating the path from occupational disability to employment — to ask broader questions about what the relational quality of public sector work requires of organisations. {{< publication-link href="/publications/een-wankel-evenwicht" value="Een wankel evenwicht" >}} investigates the governance structures shaping this domain, mapping the constraints on professional agency and the points at which targeted design intervention can create meaningful room for something different. What it takes for such interventions to gain traction without being absorbed back into the conditions they seek to contest is the concern of {{< theme-link href="/themes/stewarding-change" value="Stewarding change" >}}.
```

## Update in light of Ch.1 draft 3 (Public Design Practices)

Draft 3 sharpens the chapter considerably and changes what the public-design-practices theme page should carry. Three things in particular.

First, the **diagnosis of the problem is much more precise.** The chapter no longer rests on a generic "promising but unstable" framing; it names four interrelated problems in current discourse: fragmentation across subdisciplines defined by output rather than expertise; discursive indeterminacy about what designers know how to do; the semantic ambiguity of the word *design* itself; and a tendency to position design in a subordinate, instrumental relationship to other practices. The chapter's verdict — "thin on shared meaning, thin on record, and imperfectly attuned to the terrain" — is one of the strongest sentences in the draft and translates directly to the theme page.

Second, the **definition of public design as a transversal practice is sharper.** Draft 3: "the situated, institutionally shaped ways in which design repertoire is enacted when collaborating within the (semi-)public sector towards the creation of public value, in light of complex societal challenges." This is the formulation the theme page should carry. The transversal character is doubly elaborated — cutting across the multiple practices that constitute the public sector (politics, policymaking, administration, service delivery, enforcement, communication), and operating within an emerging design exchange field while being deployed across diverse issue fields. The current page gestures at the first but not the second.

Third, the **three contextual movements are now developed**, and the third — contesting ⇄ legitimising — names what the previous version only implied: that the design practitioner in this domain is **inevitably a political actor**, and that the field's credibility depends less on claims of methodological neutrality than on the deliberate work of holding open structural conditions long enough for them to be reconfigured. This pivots the closing sentence directly into Stewarding change, where probing and stewarding are the moves through which that holding-open is done.

### Revised suggested rewrite (replaces the earlier suggestion)

```markdown
---
title: "Public design practices"
description: "What design practices distinctively bring to complex societal challenges, and how that repertoire adapts when deployed in political-administrative contexts."
gradientFrom: "#FF4500"
gradientTo: "#FF9E00"
weight: 10
---

Design practices are increasingly recognised as having transformative potential for complex societal challenges in the public sector, yet what designers distinctively bring remains poorly articulated. Contemporary discourse tends to fragment across subdisciplines defined by their output — service, social, systemic, transition design — or to dissolve into generic qualities (creative, empathic, iterative) that say something about disposition but little about expertise. The word *design* itself is ambiguous, and "design for policy" framings cast the practice as an instrumental supplier of creativity in service to ends that others have set. Thin on shared meaning, thin on record, and imperfectly attuned to the terrain, design is poorly placed to legitimise its seat at the table — and prone to overpromising and underdelivering as a result.

The project {{< project-link href="/projects/defining-design-practices-for-societal-challenges" value="Defining design practices for societal challenges" >}} and the publication {{< publication-link href="/publications/what-do-designers-bring-to-the-table" value="What do designers bring to the table?" >}} address this at the level of *competencies* — capabilities encompassing related behaviours centred on a shared intent, at a level of abstraction that can span varied applications while remaining grounded in expertise. Four emerged as constitutive: integrating, reframing, formgiving, and orchestrating. Two structural relationships between them describe what makes design distinctive when facing complexity: the co-evolutionary search for a perspective on action, and the navigation of conflict and tension. Together, these allow a reconceptualisation of public design as a *transversal practice*: the situated, institutionally shaped ways in which design repertoire is enacted when collaborating within the (semi-)public sector towards the creation of public value, in light of complex societal challenges. Public design cuts across the multiple practices that constitute the public sector — politics, policymaking, administration, service delivery, enforcement, communication — and operates within an emerging design exchange field while being deployed across diverse issue fields.

What changes when this repertoire travels into the political-administrative domain is the nature, scale, and stakes of these movements. Three contextual oscillations describe how the practice must adapt: between idealising and realising, between complexifying and concretising, and between contesting and legitimising. The third makes explicit what the others imply: the design practitioner in this domain is inevitably a political actor, and the field's credibility depends less on claims of methodological neutrality than on the deliberate work of holding open structural conditions long enough for them to be reconfigured. How that repertoire is carried through organisational life — without being absorbed back into the logics it sets out to challenge — is the work of {{< theme-link href="/themes/stewarding-change" value="Stewarding change" >}}.
```

### A small consequence for the Stewarding change page

If the public-design-practices page closes on "holding open structural conditions long enough for them to be reconfigured," the Stewarding change page's opening can be tightened. The phrase "probing entrenched structures and carrying forward what they open up" in the earlier suggested description still holds; the body's first paragraph can drop the generic "navigating resistance, building trust" line in favour of language that connects directly to the contesting/legitimising oscillation — for example: "remaining alert to what is being reproduced and what is being transformed, particularly where the structures at issue have proven adept at incorporating critique into their own legitimation." That sentence carries the political point through from Ch.1 to Ch.3–5 without expanding the page.
