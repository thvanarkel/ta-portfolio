Collaborations between designers and public sector organisations frequently produce outcomes that are meaningful in prototype but never become embedded in practice. The gap between concept and change is a question of relational work — navigating resistance, building trust, translating intentions across roles and contexts, and attending to an initiative as the conditions around it shift. A broader research question follows from this: under what conditions can design interventions produce effects that outlast the collaboration itself, and what kinds of work does that require of designers?

The project {{< project-link href="/projects/designing-with-institutions" value="Designing with institutions" >}} follows this in real time, tracing how design interventions move through organisational life and what enables or forecloses their effects. What makes this difficult to study is also what makes it important: effects unfold slowly, unevenly, and often through paths no one planned. In {{< publication-link href="/publications/from-ideas-to-change" value="From ideas to change" >}} we identified the recurring challenges designers face across collaborations and the practices through which they respond, laying groundwork for understanding not just what goes wrong but how and under what conditions design interventions can contribute to lasting organisational change. What is ultimately at stake in those efforts is explored in {{< theme-link href="/themes/caring-organisations-and-services" value="Caring organisations & services" >}}.
