In many Dutch public sector organisations, the conditions for genuine care have been systematically eroded as a result of how those organisations are structured. The rise of New Public Management has produced governance built on control, audit, and contractual accountability. These arrangements fragment service delivery, narrow the categories within which professionals can exercise judgement, and erode the relational conditions that care requires. A central question for this theme is how design interventions can work at the level of those structures: not to optimise them, but to understand how they shape agency, and to open space—within and against existing arrangements—for more attentive and caring ways of working.

The work draws on a specific case—citizens navigating the path from occupational disability to employment—to ask broader questions about what care requires of organisations. {{< publication-link href="/publications/een-wankel-evenwicht" value="Een wankel evenwicht" >}} investigates the governance structures shaping this domain, mapping the constraints on professional agency and the points at which targeted design intervention can create meaningful room for something different. What it takes for those interventions to gain traction over time is the concern of {{< theme-link href="/themes/stewarding-change" value="Stewarding change" >}}.
