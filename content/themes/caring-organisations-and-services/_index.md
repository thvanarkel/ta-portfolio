Public sector organisations often fail to care — not because the people within them don't want to, but because of how those organisations are structured. Fragmented service delivery, contractual accountability, and governance built on distrust constrain the conditions under which professionals can exercise judgement, form relationships, and respond adequately to people's needs. This theme works at the level of those structures: not to optimise them, but to understand how they shape agency, and how design interventions can open space within and against them. The project {{< project-link href="/projects/a-precarious-equilibrium" value="A precarious equilibrium" >}} develops and tests an intervention in occupational disability services, probing what structural change is actually possible within a heavily governed system.

The work draws on a specific case — citizens navigating the transition from disability to employment — to ask broader questions about what care requires of organisations. {{< publication-link href="/publications/een-wankel-evenwicht" value="Een wankel evenwicht" >}} investigates the governance structures shaping this domain, mapping the constraints on professional agency and the points at which targeted design intervention can create meaningful room for something different. What it takes for those interventions to gain traction over time is the concern of {{< theme-link href="/themes/stewarding-change" value="Stewarding change" >}}.
