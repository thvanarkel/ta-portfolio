Design and public administration have developed distinct practices and vocabularies when thinking about and aiming for change. When they meet in the context of complex societal challenges—climate change, housing shortages, social welfare, organised crime and many more—the encounter seems promising and generative but also unstable.

In the project {{< project-link href="/projects/defining-design-practices-for-societal-challenges" value="Defining design practices for societal challenges" >}} we mapped an initial set of competencies, which we further detailed and expanded on in {{< publication-link href="/publications/what-do-designers-bring-to-the-table" value="What do designers bring to the table?" >}}. This repertoire that designers bring: reframing problems, integrating perspectives, formgiving possibilities, orchestrating change—may need to adapt to a context that operates under bureaucratic, regulatory and political structures that enable and constrain how change can occur. The public sector is a field of interconnected practices—practices such as politics, policymaking, administration, service delivery, enforcement, and communication—carrying its own logics. Design enters this field as a transversal and complementary practice: rather than being in service to any single one of these practices it cuts across them as well as complements them.

A central question that remains is what happens to that repertoire as it travels to the political-administrative domain—and whether design's claimed transformative potential holds when it enters these contexts, or whether it tends to reproduce the institutional logics it sets out to challenge. How that repertoire gets carried through the messy reality of implementation is what {{< theme-link href="/themes/stewarding-change" value="Stewarding change" >}} is about.
