Design and public administration have developed distinct practices and vocabularies when thinking about and aiming for change. When they meet in the context of complex societal challenges—in healthcare, welfare, governance, and beyond—the encounter is generative but also unstable. 

The repertoire that designers bring {{< publication-link href="/publications/what-do-designers-bring-to-the-table" value="What do designers bring to the table?" >}}: reframing problems, integrating perspectives, formgiving possibilities, orchestrating collaboration—work differently within institutional contexts structured by politics, procedure, and accountability. The public sector is a field of interconnected practices—practices such as politics, policymaking, administration, service delivery, enforcement, and communication—each carrying its own logic and material arrangements. Design enters this field as a transversal and complementary practice: it does not belong to any single cluster but cuts across them, operating differently depending on where it lands.

A central question for this theme is what designers actually bring to these collaborations, and what happens to that contribution as it travels across disciplinary and institutional boundaries. The project {{< project-link href="/projects/defining-design-practices-for-societal-challenges" value="Defining design practices for societal challenges" >}} maps that repertoire, attending to how design competencies are understood, applied, and sometimes lost in translation. {{< publication-link href="/publications/what-do-designers-bring-to-the-table" value="What do designers bring to the table?" >}} examines where those competencies take hold, where they encounter friction, and what this reveals about public design practice as an emerging field. How that repertoire gets carried through the messy reality of implementation is what {{< theme-link href="/themes/stewarding-change" value="Stewarding change" >}} is about.
