Design and public administration have developed separate vocabularies for thinking about change. When they meet on complex societal challenges — in healthcare, welfare, governance, and beyond — the encounter is generative but also unstable. Design's strengths (reframing problems, prototyping possibilities, orchestrating collaboration) operate differently within institutional contexts structured by politics, procedure, and accountability. The project {{< project-link href="/projects/defining-design-practices-for-societal-challenges" value="Defining design practices for societal challenges" >}} maps what designers actually bring to these collaborations and how those contributions are understood and applied across disciplinary and institutional boundaries.

A deeper question runs through this work: whether design's claimed transformative potential is genuinely distinct, or whether it tends to reproduce the institutional logics it sets out to challenge. {{< publication-link href="/publications/what-do-designers-bring-to-the-table" value="What do designers bring to the table?" >}} examines how design competencies travel — where they take hold, where they find friction, and what this reveals about public design practice as an emerging field. How that repertoire gets carried through the messy reality of implementation is what {{< theme-link href="/themes/stewarding-change" value="Stewarding change" >}} is about.
