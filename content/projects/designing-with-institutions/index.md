Public organisations in the Netherlands are under mounting strain, and these strains are symptoms of a particular way of organising, one that treats inherently complex social systems as though they were simple and controllable. Design practices are suggested to bring a valuable repertoire to changing such systems and thereby addressing the complex societal challenges facing the public sector, yet their impact in the public domain remains contested. Design outcomes often stay small, conceptual, and local, detached from organisational reality and rarely carried through into lasting change. Part of the difficulty is that (external) designers tend to approach public organisations as monolithic, inertial entities that mostly resist change, with limited grasp of the political-administrative dynamics that both enable and constrain transformation.

The project explores how design can engage more effectively with institutions: the rules, norms, shared meanings, and relational patterns that guide behaviour in public organisations. It adopts a "designing with institutions" perspective, recognising that institutions are constituted through everyday organisational practice. Design interventions do not simply operate within institutional contexts; they participate in the ongoing reproduction, and the potential transformation, of institutional arrangements. The structures through which organisations work, sedimented in regulations, IT systems, routines, professional dispositions, and worldviews, become the object of design, rather than a background to design around.

Working with these structures depends on first making them legible enough to be moved deliberately, and on stewarding change: carrying a design initiative forward through organisational contingencies, deciding when to reframe, redirect, sustain, or let go as obstacles and opportunities emerge. Through longitudinal engagement with four public design initiatives—Kopkrakers, Fair Practice Lab, Rijksmakerstaat, and a project with UWV—the project examines the trajectory from initiation through implementation to institutionalisation. It traces how interventions can balance challenging existing structures with reproducing the beneficial aspects of current arrangements, and how localised experiments might travel across contexts. The aim is to generate knowledge, frameworks, tools, and methods that support designers in engaging these structural factors, contributing to more effective design practice for transforming organisational practices in the public sector.
